    Multi-Head Attention and Layer Stacking

    How do multiple Attention Heads and stacked layers construct deep semantic reasoning?

    Why Do We Need Multiple Attention Heads?

    In the previous lesson, we saw how the Self-Attention Mechanism identifies relationships between words in a sentence. However, human language is highly complex. A single word in a sentence can simultaneously be involved in multiple grammatical and semantic relationships.

    Consider the sentence: "The cute dog chased the cat because it was running fast."

    Focus on the word dog:

    • What kind of dog? - A "cute" dog (Adjective-Noun relationship).
    • What did the dog do? - It "chased" (Subject-Verb relationship).
    • Why did it chase? - Because the cat was running away (Causal relationship).

    If we had only a single attention mechanism (called Single-Head Attention), the model would have to average all these diverse relationships into a single attention representation. This averaging would wash away the subtle nuances, causing the model to miss key connections.

    To solve this, Multi-Head Attention was introduced. Multiple "Attention Heads" are executed in parallel. Each Head independently learns to focus on different aspects of the relationships:

    • Head 1 (Subject-Verb): Focuses on subject-verb relationships (e.g., dog & chased).
    • Head 2 (Pronoun Resolution): Resolves pronoun references (e.g., identifying whether "it" refers to the dog or the cat).
    • Head 3 (Modifier/Adjective): Identifies adjectives and the nouns they modify (e.g., cute & dog).
    • Head 4 (Positional Context): Tracks word sequence and local syntax patterns.

    Dimension Splitting: The Mathematical Configuration of Heads

    At first glance, running multiple heads in parallel might seem computationally expensive. However, the Transformer manages this process incredibly efficiently.

    Suppose our model's embedding dimension (d_model) is 512, and we have 8 attention heads (h).

    Instead of letting each head process the full 512 dimensions, the model splits the dimension into 8 parts:

    d_head = d_model / h = 512 / 8 = 64

    Each head computes attention in a smaller subspace of only 64 dimensions (d_head). Finally, the 64-dimensional outputs from all 8 heads are concatenated back into the original 512 dimensions.

    This allows us to analyze the text from 8 different semantic perspectives simultaneously, without increasing computational cost!

    Layer Stacking: Unlocking Depth Layer by Layer

    A Transformer does not contain just a single block of Self-Attention and Feed-Forward Networks. These blocks are stacked on top of each other in a process called Layer Stacking.

    For example, LLaMA-3-8B has 32 layers, and GPT-4 has well over 100 layers. Stacking allows the model to build hierarchical representations from raw text to deep conceptual understanding:

    Top Layers (Layers 9-12): Global Reasoning & Completion
    Deals with abstract concepts, reasoning, sentiment, and final next-token predictions.
    ↓
    Middle Layers (Layers 5-8): Syntax & Relations
    Processes grammatical structures, clause connections, and pronoun resolution.
    ↓
    Bottom Layers (Layers 1-4): Surface Lexical Features
    Focuses on basic parts of speech, spelling, and local word sequences.
    Middle Layers (Layers 5-8): Syntax & Relations

    Primary Responsibility: Analyzing grammatical syntax and connections between different clauses.
