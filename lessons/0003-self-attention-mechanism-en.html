Lesson 3

Self-Attention Mechanism

How does a word extract the correct context from its surrounding words?

The Context Problem

Consider a common English word like bank. It can have two completely different meanings:

Sentence 1: "I deposited my money at the bank."
👉 Here, bank refers to a financial institution (Financial Institution).

Sentence 2: "We sat on the muddy bank of the river."
👉 Here, bank refers to the side of a river (Riverbank).

As humans, we read the entire sentence and instantly understand the difference. But how does a computer understand? To a computer, each word is just a lifeless list of numbers (Embedding Vector).

Popular architectures before the Transformer (such as RNNs or LSTMs) processed words sequentially (one by one). Consequently, if a sentence was too long, the context of the early words would fail to reach the end.

The revolutionary solution to this problem is the Transformer's Self-Attention Mechanism. It allows each word to establish a direct connection with all other words in the sentence, perfectly extracting context-dependent meanings.

An Analogy for Query, Key, and Value

Self-Attention performs all calculations using three vectors: Query (Q), Key (K), and Value (V). To understand them, we can use a **YouTube Search Engine** analogy:

🔍
Query (Q)

The search term you type into the YouTube search bar (e.g., "Deep Learning Course"). This is your Query.

Word Perspective: "Which other words in the sentence do I need to relate to?"

🏷️
Key (K)

The titles, tags, or descriptions of the videos hosted on YouTube. This is the Key.

Word Perspective: "Why should other words pay attention to me? This is a label of what information I contain."

🎞️
Value (V)

The actual video content that you play once your Query matches a video's Key. This is the Value.

Word Perspective: "If a relationship is established with me, what actual semantic content or information will I provide?"

The Transformer multiplies each input word's embedding vector by three distinct weight matrices (learned during training) to produce the Q, K, and V vectors.

How Self-Attention Works (4 Easy Steps)

Let's break down the mathematical process in simple terms without complex formulas:

Step 1: Similarity (Dot Product)

The Query of the word we want to analyze is dotted with the Keys of every word in the sentence (Dot Product). The dot product mathematically measures how aligned or similar two vectors are. The result is called the **Raw Score**.

Step 2: Scaling

If the vector size or dimensionality ($d_k$) is very large, the dot product scores can grow extremely large. To keep these values stable, they are divided by $\sqrt{d_k}$.

Step 3: Softmax (Attention Weights)

The scaled scores are passed through a Softmax function. Softmax normalizes all scores into fractions between 0 and 1 that sum up to exactly 1 (or 100%). These fractions are the **Attention Weights**, telling the model what percentage of attention should be paid to each word.

Step 4: Weighted Sum of Values

Finally, the Value ($V$) vector of each word is multiplied by its corresponding Attention Weight, and these weighted vectors are added together (**Weighted Sum**). This combined vector is the final, context-rich representation of the word!
